## Key Findings

"Workforce agility" is an established construct in organizational and management research, though the literature explicitly notes a lack of a single consensus definition. A defensible synthesis for the paper:

- *Seminal definition.* Breu, Hemingway, Strathern & Bridger (2002) derived workforce agility from organizational-agility characteristics and identified five acquired capabilities of an agile workforce: intelligence, competencies, collaboration, culture, and information systems. This is the foundational and most-cited source (cited 763).
- *Dimensional consensus.* Recent systematic reviews converge on a multi-dimensional construct: proactivity, flexibility/adaptability, resilience, and competence (Tessarini Junior & Saltorato 2021), evolving from an original "multitasking" focus toward adaptability, proactivity, and resilience (Alviani et al. 2024).
- *Attitude-behavior-ability framing.* Muduli & Choudhury (2025) define it across agile attitude, behavior, and ability; Salmen & Festing (2022) note the field's "great variety of definitions" and propose a combined ability + behavioral definition.
- *Operations/management framing.* Qin & Nembhard (2015) define workforce agility as the management of labor capacity and capability for organizations facing unpredictable change; Sherehiy et al. (2007) situate it within enterprise agility (adaptability + flexibility).
- *Multilevel.* Nayak & Sahoo (2026) frame it as a multilevel construct (individual, group, organizational); most empirical work is at the individual level (Alviani et al. 2024).

**For the paper (T#1):** Define workforce agility, citing Breu et al. (2002) as the origin and a recent systematic review (e.g., Tessarini & Saltorato 2021 or Salmen & Festing 2022) for the proactivity/adaptability/resilience/competence dimensions. Note explicitly that the literature lacks a single consensus definition, which justifies adopting an operational one for this Viewpoint.
