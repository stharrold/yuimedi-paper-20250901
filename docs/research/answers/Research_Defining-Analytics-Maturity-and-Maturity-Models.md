## Key Findings

"Analytics maturity" is a well-established construct in information-systems and management research, operationalized through numerous maturity models (well beyond healthcare's HIMSS AMAM). This directly answers Reviewer T#1's request for a literature-grounded definition.

- *Canonical definition of analytics.* The widely reused base definition is Davenport & Harris (2007): "the extensive use of data, statistical and quantitative analysis, explanatory and predictive models, and fact-based management to drive decisions and actions" (cited via Lismont et al. 2017).
- *Definition of analytics maturity.* Halper (TDWI, 2020) defines analytics maturity as "the evolution of an organization to integrate, manage, and leverage all relevant internal and external data sources into key decision points," involving technology, resources, data management, governance, and organizational components.
- *Maturity models are the standard operationalization.* Grossman (2018) introduces the Analytic Processes Maturity Model (APMM) with five levels (from building reports to strategy-driven enterprise-wide analytics). Lismont et al. (2017) use the DELTA model (Data, Enterprise, Leadership, Targets, Analysts) and find four empirical maturity clusters.
- *The construct is multi-dimensional and reviewed systematically.* Krol & Zdonek (2020) compare 11 analytics maturity models; Langer (2025) systematically reviews 38 data-and-analytics maturity models and classifies them as organization-oriented, technology-oriented, or data-oriented. Chen & Nath (2018) enumerate common dimensions: information quality, master data management, data-warehousing architecture, analytics capabilities, strategic intent, technology sophistication, people (skills/culture), and process improvement.

**For the paper (T#1):** Define analytics maturity citing Davenport & Harris (2007) for "analytics" and a maturity-model source (Grossman 2018 APMM, or Langer 2025 / Krol & Zdonek 2020 for the model landscape). This explicitly situates the construct in the IS literature rather than relying on HIMSS AMAM alone, which also pre-empts Reviewer Q#1's self-selection-bias critique of relying solely on HIMSS certification data.
